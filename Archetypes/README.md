## What Archetypes Are

An **archetype** is a recurring pattern of human experience—a role, tension, or transformation that appears across individuals, cultures, and time periods. Archetypes are not stereotypes (superficial categories) but **deep structures** of consciousness and behavior.

Think of them as:
- **Psychological templates** (Jung's collective unconscious)
- **Narrative patterns** (Campbell's hero's journey)
- **Behavioral scripts** (repeated across contexts)
- **Existential positions** (ways of being-in-the-world)

Archetypes are **trans-personal**—they're not about individual psychology alone, but about patterns that transcend the individual while manifesting through them.

---

## Why Archetypes Matter

When you recognize an archetype operating in yourself or others, you gain:

1. **Predictive power** — You can anticipate likely behaviors, tensions, outcomes
2. **Explanatory depth** — You understand *why* someone acts a certain way (not just *what* they do)
3. **Intervention points** — You can see where change is possible and where resistance will occur
4. **Compassion** — Recognizing shared patterns reduces judgment ("they're not broken—they're in a pattern")
5. **Self-knowledge** — Seeing your own archetypes lets you choose consciously instead of reacting automatically

---

## The Method: How Archetypes Are Identified

Archetypes in this repository are **inductively derived**—built from observation, not imposed top-down from theory.

### The Process:

1. **Observe** — Notice recurring patterns in lived experience (self, others, culture, history)
2. **Abstract** — Extract the general structure from specific instances
3. **Analyze** — Break down the pattern into components (thoughts, behaviors, constraints, autonomies)
4. **Document** — Record it systematically so it's searchable, comparable, expandable
5. **Test** — Look for the pattern in new contexts. Does it hold? Where does it break?
6. **Refine** — Update the archetype as understanding deepens

**This is phenomenology meets systems thinking**—observing what *is*, then mapping the underlying structure.

## The Structure: How Archetypes Are Documented

Every archetype in this folder follows a consistent template to enable comparison and connection. Each entry includes:

### Core Components:
- **Overview** — What is this pattern?
- **Philosophical Core** — What fundamental tension or question does it embody?
- **Psychoanalytic Structure** — The internal mechanics (thoughts, behaviors, constraints, autonomies)
- **Probabilities & Uncertainties** — Likely outcomes and unknowables
- **Anomalies** — Exceptions, contradictions, edge cases
- **Interconnections** — How it relates to other archetypes
- **Catalog** — Real examples across contexts
- **Practice** — How to work with this pattern (if you're in it, supporting someone in it, or observing it)

This structure allows you to:
- **Compare** archetypes side-by-side
- **Map** connections and feedback loops
- **Deepen** understanding over time
- **Apply** insights to real situations

## How to Use This Folder

**If you're new to archetypal thinking:**  
Start with **The Wounded Hermit** or **The Trapped Sovereign**—they're foundational and widely recognizable.

**If you're looking for a specific pattern:**  
Use the catalog tags (at the bottom of each archetype) to search by theme (e.g., isolation, transformation, guilt).

**If you're analyzing yourself or someone else:**  
Read multiple archetypes—people rarely embody just one. Look for **combinations** and **tensions** between archetypes.

**If you're contributing:**  
Use the `_template.md` file to document a new archetype. Ensure it's grounded in observation, not speculation.

## Key Principles

### 1. Archetypes Are Patterns, Not People
A person isn't "The Wounded Hermit." A person **is in** the Wounded Hermit pattern (for now, in this context, to some degree).

People move between archetypes. Archetypes combine. Archetypes evolve.

### 2. Archetypes Are Descriptive, Not Prescriptive
They describe what *is*, not what *should be*. No archetype is inherently good or bad—each serves a function, has costs and benefits.

### 3. Archetypes Are Dynamic, Not Static
People aren't locked into patterns. Archetypes show **probable trajectories**, but consciousness and choice can shift them.

### 4. Archetypes Are Trans-Cultural
While specific expressions vary, the underlying patterns recur. The Wounded Hermit appears in ancient mythology and modern therapy. The Trapped Sovereign shows up in folk tales and contemporary poverty.

### 5. Archetypes Interconnect
No archetype exists in isolation. They form systems—reinforcing, contradicting, transforming into each other. See `/Maps/` for these interconnections.
